---

Portugal is the eurozone's quiet outperformer—posting the highest quarterly growth in Q3 2025 (+0.8%) while larger economies stagnate. Like Spain, Portugal benefits from services orientation, tourism strength, and demographic improvement through migration. Unlike Spain, Portugal's small size limits both upside and downside.

The economy has reduced long-standing vulnerabilities that characterized the 2008-2014 crisis period. Fiscal position has improved. Financial sector has stabilized. Growth, while modest in absolute terms, represents meaningful improvement from the decade of austerity.

**Growth drivers**:
- Services-oriented economy with strong tourism sector
- Net migration improving demographic outlook
- Household consumption accelerating
- Reduced macro-financial vulnerabilities

**Growth constraints**:
- Small economy (GDP ~$280 billion) limits scale effects
- Low-value-added jobs constrain productivity
- Weak investment relative to peers
- Limited domestic AI/technology development

**Comparison with Spain**: Portugal shares Spain's growth model and outperforms on recent quarters, but Spain's 4x larger economy provides resilience Portugal lacks. Both face the same structural challenge: services growth without productivity transformation.

**Near term (2026-2028)**: Growth continues in 1.9-2.2% range. Tourism remains strong. Household consumption supports domestic demand.

**Mid term (2029-2032)**: Test of sustainability. Either: Portugal maintains growth trajectory through continued services strength; or structural constraints (productivity, investment) manifest as slowdown.

**Late term (2033-2036)**: Portugal's position stabilizes. Success: Sustained 2%+ growth positions Portugal as eurozone bright spot. Risk: Without productivity transformation, growth remains below potential for AI-era competition.

**Classification rationale**: "Survivor" because Portugal grows adequately and improves from crisis-era lows, but small scale and structural constraints prevent transformation into AI-era winner.
