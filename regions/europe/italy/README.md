# Italy: 2026-2036

> **Outlook**: Declining
> **Growth Outlook**: Low/Negative
> **Constraint**: Debt; demographics; manufacturing crisis

## December 2025 Reality

Italy's economy struggles at the margin of growth and stagnation:

| Metric | Status |
|--------|--------|
| **GDP growth 2025** | 0.4% projected |
| **Q3 2025 GDP** | +0.1% (erasing -0.1% Q2 contraction) |
| **Debt-to-GDP** | 138.2% (among eurozone's highest) |
| **AI adoption** | 8.2% of enterprises |
| **Digital intensity** | 70.2% of SMEs at basic level |
| **RRF digital allocation** | 25.6% of €62.3B |

**Manufacturing**: Once Italy's growth engine, now in "deep crisis" facing global competition, high labor costs, and innovation lag.

---

Italy presents a case of gradual decline rather than acute crisis—the slow erosion of a major economy that can neither reform decisively nor collapse dramatically. Growth oscillates around zero, debt accumulates, AI adoption lags, and manufacturing loses competitiveness. The trajectory is negative but stable—Italy will be poorer relative to peers in 2036 but will not have fractured.

The manufacturing crisis is structural. Italian manufacturing, which powered the postwar economic miracle, faces global competition it cannot meet. High labor costs, insufficient technology investment, and fragmented firm structure (heavily SME-weighted) prevent the productivity gains that would restore competitiveness. Value added contracted in Q3 2025 for the industrial sector.

AI adoption reflects broader technology lag. Only 8.2% of Italian enterprises have adopted AI—far below European leaders and dramatically below the US or Asian competitors. While 70.2% of SMEs achieve basic digital intensity, "basic" is insufficient in an AI-driven economy. Italy holds position in quantum and semiconductors but commercial AI deployment lags.

The RRF (Recovery and Resilience Facility) offers a lifeline. Italy has received €60 billion in disbursements, with 25.6% directed toward digital transformation and 39% toward climate goals. This represents genuine investment that could shift trajectory—if execution matches allocation. Italian history suggests execution risk is significant.

Debt at 138.2% of GDP constrains all options. Italy cannot spend its way to growth; interest costs already consume substantial revenue. Any crisis—Italian or European—that raises spreads threatens fiscal stability.

**Why "Declining" rather than "Fracturing"**:
- Social cohesion remains stronger than UK or France
- No comparable demographic fractures or unassimilated populations
- Regional identity provides stability (North-South divide is old and managed)
- EU membership provides institutional anchor
- Rate of decline is slow enough for adaptation

**Near term (2026-2028)**: RRF investment continues. Growth remains in 0.5-1.5% range. Manufacturing restructuring proceeds slowly. Unemployment continues gradual decline (5.9% projected by 2027).

**Mid term (2029-2032)**: RRF spending phases out. Italy must demonstrate organic growth capacity. Either: digital investments yield productivity gains or trajectory reverts to stagnation.

**Late term (2033-2036)**: Italy's position clarifies. Either modest growth (1-2%) validates transformation efforts, or chronic stagnation (0-1%) confirms structural decline. Neither scenario involves crisis; both involve reduced relative standing.

**For individuals**: Italy offers high quality of life, strong regional cultures, and stability—at the cost of limited economic opportunity. Careers benefit from EU mobility; domestic prospects remain constrained.
