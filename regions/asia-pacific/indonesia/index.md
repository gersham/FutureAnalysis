# Indonesia: 2026-2036

> **Outlook**: Survivor
> **Growth Outlook**: Moderate
> **Constraint**: Infrastructure; energy; education; geographic fragmentation

*Synthesis of: [Data](data/) | [Trends](trends/) | [Analysis](analysis/)*

---

## December 2025 Reality

Indonesia's AI ambitions face infrastructure reality:

| Metric | Status |
|--------|--------|
| **Data center capacity** | 307-500 MW (vs Malaysia 1.3 GW) |
| **Energy mix** | 60% coal dependent |
| **GDP growth 2025** | 5.1-5.4% |
| **Population** | 280+ million |
| **Grid reliability outside Java** | Problematic |

**Critical gap**: Data center capacity of 307-500 MW cannot support enterprise AI at scale. Malaysia has 1.3 GW; Singapore has 1 GW; Indonesia lags significantly.

## Infrastructure Reality

### Energy

Indonesia remains 60% coal dependent. While the RUPTL 2025-2034 targets 42.6 GW of new renewable capacity, fossil fuel generation is also planned to grow 40% in the same period. The grid cannot currently support AI-scale data center expansion outside Java.

The World Bank's September 2025 $500 million I-ENET programme aims to modernize the Java-Madura-Bali grid—covering 160 million people but leaving the other 120 million Indonesians with unreliable power.

### Data Centers

Indonesia's data center market is growing but from a low base:
- Current capacity: 307-500 MW (primarily Jakarta)
- Projected 2030: 3.56 GW IT load capacity
- Major projects underway (DCI's 600 MW facility)
- But: Malaysia already has 1.3 GW; Indonesia is playing catch-up

Major hyperscalers (Google, AWS, Microsoft) are investing—Microsoft committed $1.7 billion over four years. But Indonesia competes with Singapore, Malaysia, and even Thailand for regional data center investment.

### Geographic Challenge

17,504 islands create infrastructure challenges unique to Indonesia:
- Grid connectivity outside Java is poor
- Logistics are complex and expensive
- Digital inclusion varies dramatically by location
- Development concentrated in Java (~56% of population, higher share of GDP)

## What Indonesia Has

### Natural Resources: The Compute-Era Advantage

Indonesia controls **40% of global nickel reserves**—the single most important battery material for the AI and electrification era. As AI-powered economies accelerate and consume more energy storage, Indonesian nickel becomes increasingly strategic. This is the "resources for compute" dynamic in action: Indonesia may not build AI systems, but it supplies what AI systems need.

| Resource | Indonesia's Position | AI-Era Relevance |
|----------|---------------------|------------------|
| **Nickel** | 40% of global reserves; world's largest producer | Critical for EV batteries, grid storage, electronics |
| **Copper** | Significant deposits (Grasberg) | Essential for data centers, power transmission |
| **Coal** | Major exporter | Powers domestic grid; export revenue |
| **Tin** | Major producer | Electronics manufacturing |
| **Bauxite** | Large deposits | Aluminum for infrastructure |

Indonesia has pursued a **downstream processing strategy**—banning raw nickel ore exports since 2020 to force smelting and battery production domestically. This has attracted Chinese investment (CATL, battery supply chains) but also created tension with Western supply chain diversification efforts. The strategy's success depends on whether Indonesia can convert resource leverage into sustained industrial development or becomes primarily an extraction site for Chinese supply chains.

The resource dividend operates independently of Indonesia's AI adoption capacity. As AI-accelerated economies grow, they consume more nickel, copper, and energy. Indonesia benefits from this demand whether or not it captures first-order AI productivity gains.

### Scale

280 million people is both advantage and challenge:
- Large domestic market for digital services
- But: large uneducated population creates demographic liability dynamics
- Education system produces insufficient skilled workers (9 million digital worker gap by 2030)

### Regional Position

Indonesia's location and market size make it important regardless of AI capture:
- Largest ASEAN economy
- Strategic location for maritime trade
- Investor interest due to market potential

## Outlook Assessment

### Near Term (2026-2028)

- GDP growth of 4-5% on commodities and domestic consumption
- Data center capacity expands but remains behind regional peers
- AI adoption grows in Jakarta formal sector; limited elsewhere
- Grid modernization begins on Java

### Mid Term (2029-2032)

- Infrastructure investments bear fruit (if executed)
- Data center capacity approaches 2-3 GW
- AI productivity gains visible in formal sector
- Gap between Java and outer islands widens

### Late Term (2033-2036)

**Optimistic scenario**: Infrastructure investments succeed. Data centers scale. AI productivity gains spread beyond Jakarta. GDP growth sustains 4-5%. Indonesia benefits from commodity demand for AI hardware (nickel).

**Base case**: Indonesia muddles through. Jakarta/Java captures some AI gains. Outer islands lag. GDP growth of 3-4%. Not a winner but not declining—the classic survivor profile.

**Risk scenario**: Infrastructure gaps persist. AI investment flows to Singapore/Malaysia instead. Indonesia remains commodity-dependent. Demographic dividend becomes liability as educated jobs don't scale.

---

## Classification Rationale

**"Survivor"** rather than "Winner" because:

1. **Infrastructure gap**: Data center capacity (307-500 MW) far behind regional peers
2. **Energy constraints**: 60% coal; unreliable grid outside Java
3. **Geographic fragmentation**: 17,504 islands make scaling difficult
4. **Education gap**: 9 million digital worker shortage
5. **Demographic risk**: Large, relatively uneducated population

Indonesia has resources, scale, and strategic location that prevent decline. But infrastructure and human capital constraints limit AI gain capture. Indonesia will survive the AI transition; whether it thrives depends on execution that Indonesia has not historically demonstrated.
